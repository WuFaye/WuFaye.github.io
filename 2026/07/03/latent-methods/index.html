Latent Predictive Learning: a compact map

发表于 2026-07-03 分类于 Summary

only common methods; no experiment-specific setting, no private result

One line map

Most latent predictive learning methods are answering five questions:

  1. how to encode history into a query latent $q$
  2. how to compress a future window into a target latent $k$
  3. what supervision to use: contrastive or predictive
  4. whether to inject static prior
  5. where hard negatives come from

History side: causal context encoder

Given history embeddings $x_{1:T}$, a causal encoder only allows token $t$ to attend to $\le t$:

$$ h_{1:T} = \mathrm{Enc}_{\mathrm{causal}}(x_{1:T}), \qquad q = f(h_T) $$

Typical choice:

  • causal Transformer
  • last valid hidden state as summary
  • MLP head to latent space

Essence:

  • compress the past into a state representation
  • keep temporal direction correct

Pros / cons / indication:

  • good for progression / forecasting
  • natural for variable-length sequence
  • but summary can over-focus on the last step

Future side: how to define the target

The future is often a short window $y_{1:W}$, but the model needs a single target vector.

Mean pooling

$$ k = \frac{1}{W}\sum_{w=1}^{W} y_w $$

  • pros: stable, cheap
  • cons: no order, over-smoothing

Attention pooling

$$ \alpha_w = \mathrm{softmax}(u^\top \tanh(W y_w)), \qquad k = \sum_w \alpha_w y_w $$

  • pros: focus on salient future step
  • cons: still shallow

Transformer over future window

$$ \tilde y_{1:W} = \mathrm{Enc}(y_{1:W}+p_{1:W}), \qquad k = \mathrm{Pool}(\tilde y_{1:W}) $$

  • pros: keeps local order and interaction
  • cons: heavier, not always necessary for tiny window

Latent-query cross attention

Use a few learnable latent queries $z_{1:m}$ to read future tokens:

$$ \hat z = \mathrm{CrossAttn}(z, y_{1:W}), \qquad k = \mathrm{Pool}(\hat z) $$

  • pros: latent-slot summarization, more expressive
  • cons: more parameters, less transparent

Gated multi-summary

$$ k = g_1 k_{\text{mean}} + g_2 k_{\text{last}} + g_3 k_{\text{attn}}, \qquad g = \mathrm{softmax}(W[\cdot]) $$

  • pros: adaptive bias-variance tradeoff
  • cons: can collapse to one branch

Contrastive predictive objective

The core InfoNCE form is:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{NCE}} = - \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \log \frac{\exp(q_i^\top k_i / \tau)} {\sum_{j=1}^{B}\exp(q_i^\top k_j / \tau)} $$

Interpretation:

  • positive pair: same sample's history and future
  • negatives: mismatched futures
  • the task is retrieval in latent space

Pros / cons / indication:

  • no manual label required
  • geometry is clean and easy to evaluate
  • but quality depends heavily on negatives

Hard negatives

Random negatives are often too easy. Hard-negative methods sample futures that are close in one sense but different in another.

Retrieval bank / ANN / FAISS

Build a bank of normalized vectors and search nearest neighbors by cosine similarity:

$$ s(q,k) = \frac{q^\top k}{\|q\|\|k\|} $$

  • essence: mine confusing candidates efficiently
  • best for: dense embedding space where in-batch negatives are weak

Typical hardness pattern

  • same current state, different future
  • same diagnosis / template, different progression
  • semantically close but future-discordant

This changes the question from “is this future random?” to “which future is truly mine among plausible alternatives?”

MoCo-style queue

Maintain a momentum encoder and a FIFO queue:

$$ \theta_k \leftarrow m \theta_k + (1-m)\theta_q $$

  • pros: more negatives without huge batch
  • cons: stale keys, extra machinery

Static conditioning

Dynamic history does not contain everything. Static signals can be injected in several ways.

Prefix fusion

  • turn static summaries into prefix tokens
  • essence: static information participates inside attention

Concatenation fusion

$$ \tilde x_t = [x_t ; s] $$

  • pros: simple
  • cons: crude repetition of the same static vector

Gated latent fusion

$$ g = \sigma(W[z_{\text{dyn}}; z_{\text{static}}]), \qquad z = g \odot z_{\text{dyn}} + (1-g)\odot z_{\text{static}} $$

  • pros: explicit balance between static and dynamic
  • cons: interaction happens late

FiLM modulation

Use static latent $s$ to modulate dynamic latent $z$:

$$ \gamma = 1 + \Delta_\gamma(s), \qquad \beta = \Delta_\beta(s), \qquad \tilde z = \gamma \odot z + \beta $$

  • essence: bias dynamic representation by feature-wise scaling and shifting
  • pros: lightweight, controllable
  • cons: assumes modulation is mostly affine

Prototype prior

Learn prototype matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ and softly assign static latent $s$:

$$ a = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{sP^\top}{\tau_p}\right), \qquad p = aP $$

Then fuse prototype prior $p$ with dynamic latent.

Residual form:

$$ z = z_{\text{dyn}} + \alpha p $$

  • essence: convert heterogeneous static covariates into a mixture of latent archetypes
  • pros: interpretable prior, good for heterogeneous populations
  • cons: prototype collapse / underuse must be monitored

JEPA / BYOL-style predictive latent learning

This family removes explicit negatives. An online network predicts the future latent produced by a target network:

$$ z_c = f_{\theta}(x_{1:T}), \qquad \hat z_f = g_{\theta}(z_c), \qquad z_f = f_{\xi}(y_{1:W}) $$

Loss is usually cosine prediction:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} = 2 - 2 \cos(\hat z_f, \mathrm{sg}(z_f)) $$

Target parameters are updated by EMA:

$$ \xi \leftarrow m \xi + (1-m)\theta $$

  • pros: avoids negative-sampling sensitivity
  • cons: collapse prevention is subtle
  • best for: when negatives are noisy or hard to define

A compact method map

In latent predictive learning, methods usually modify exactly one of these layers:

  1. context encoder: causal Transformer, last-state summary
  2. future target constructor: mean / attention / Transformer / latent queries
  3. training signal: InfoNCE vs predictive cosine
  4. conditioning path: prefix / concat / gate / FiLM / prototype prior
  5. negative source: in-batch, bank-mined, queue-based

So the real question is not “which model is best?” but:

  • do we need stronger temporal modeling?
  • do we need a better future summary?
  • do we need sharper negatives?
  • do we need static prior?
  • do we want ranking supervision or teacher-target prediction?

That is the main backbone behind most latent trajectory learning variants.
